# Awake in the Floating City: A Novel by Kwan Susanna

A lot of this novel is about caregiving, and given that I live in an old people's home, I'm surrounded by caregivers and people being given care. It is a highly mundane existence. It's about feeding people and dressing them and washing them and responding to them when they get angry and being nice to them no matter what and keeping them neat and pretty and happy. But by itself, it's just about feeding and taking care of people, and by itself it's quite mundane and boring.

The book is also about the creation of a work of art.The work takes the whole book to create, and then it's performed one night, late at night, and then it disappears. I find this very interesting that the author of the book should highlight an effort, a very long effort that just lasts an hour or so, a short amount of time, and then disappears. At the same time, the author has spent a long time and gone through a long work that is intended to last for a long time because that is what books are meant to do. So the thing that she praises is not what she's doing. It's the opposite of it.

I am not much of a science fiction reader these days, so I don't know how much fantasy or magic is allowed in a good science fiction work of science fiction. None the less, this book has a lot of what I would consider as magic or fantasy things that stretch beyond what could happen in a regular world.

For example, the book is very much about life in buildings that are over 100 stories tall.Numerous buildings like this around Chinatown and downtown San Francisco.I find this very interesting because currently San Francisco has, for the most part, a 40 ft height limit.So between the time of this book, which may be 2060 or something, and now, which is 2026, a lot of buildings had to be built, and then a huge law had to be changed. That seems a bit unlikely.

The other thing is that the novel always talks about flooding and the ground floors of buildings being flooded, and streets being flooded. If you lived in San Francisco, you know it's quite hilly. Even Chinatown, which is quite close to the bay, is still probably 20 or 30 ft above sea level, and parts of Chinatown are significantly higher. You're talking about how the world water level of the world would have to have risen many feet in order to flood the ground floors of buildings in San Francisco. But yes, maybe the rain did something like flood all the ground floors or something like that, but which I didn't really notice.

Given the mundanity of the book, I read it quite quickly and skipped quite a bit. Most of it was about cleaning and bodies, the Mia's body and things like that, but then occasionally, interestingly, the writing would become flowery for a paragraph or two and then go back to being mundane.It made me wonder if somehow AI was being used in part of this.

Eddie and Jensen and the other men pretty much mostly came over as nothing burgers.

A lot of the artistic material came from a museum or library housed in one of the flooded buildings. Interesting: the library lent Bo, the protagonist, an overhead projector and lots of what seemed to me original works. This is not what libraries normally do. They usually hold onto this stuff. You're allowed to make a copy, and that's about it, but also having an overhead projector in 2060 seems unlikely.

The work of art involves some drone-like, bird-like things and were meant to light up the whole city and/or different parts of the city, and would have required fairly strong projectors and lighting systems in order to make it work. All of this was way beyond any technology I could imagine happening in the near future.

One of the most difficult things to keep operating in a tall building is the elevator. Any movement or change in pitch and things like that can stop elevators, but all the elevators mostly seem to be working except when there's no electricity.But again, this is sort of the magic fantasy kind of thing, and I mostly just accepted it.What it did somehow to track from having a classy, truly science fiction book

The descriptions of the city seem to be much more out of a tourist book than from personal experience. It thinks like talking about Alcatraz or Sam Wu's Chinese restaurant, which supposedly had a rude waiter that was fun to listen to. Only these are typical standard tourist book fare. We didn't get any insights into real Chinese cooking or dive bars in Chinatown or naughty things happening or whatever.Some of the descriptions of Chinese American life seem moderately realistic.But a lot of it was much more the story of Chinese American people born in America, not living in Chinatown but living somewhere else.

I suppose the description of creating a work of art was reasonably well described. It's always back and forth, up and down, push and pull, happy and unhappy, move forward and move backward.

There were a lot of rooftop markets and bridges at high levels between the buildings, and all sorts of things have been constructed after the flooding story. That raises all sorts of issues about how you get food delivered up to the top floors of 100-storey buildings when there aren't really many elevators and things. How do you get building materials up so you can build the bridges? Does anybody inspect these and blah blah blah? Again, a lot of this was fantastic magic stuff.

So, overall, I would give the book a fairly middling rating. Not as much fun as automatic noodle. Actually, there was very little fun in the book.

As I said, it's mostly about taking care of somebody who is getting old, and then in the end they die anyway.

So what about the main character, Bo, So she starts the book not wanting to move. She wavers a bit and thinks about moving, and then in the end she doesn't move. She gets one simple request to help somebody, and then ends up devoting her life for over a year to that person just because of that simple request.She doesn't decide to do it, but once it is done, then she sticks to it.

I suppose the only growth is that she learns about that eddie, or the boyfriend helps her develop some new technology, and she does learn how to use it and makes a piece that probably he wouldn't have even considered.But this just happens. We don't understand really her thinking about how it happens and how she tackled it.

I don't think we ever get a really clear picture why Bo stays in San Francisco, even though she's offered passage to a much nicer way of life. It just seems that she's a person who sticks to her guns.

Mia, the other main character, was supposed to be some kind of exceptional person, but pretty much her story reminded me of the stories of many Chinese immigrants living in the city Came over from China, had much, typically, but that ultimately succeeded.
