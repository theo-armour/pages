# Long-Term Investor: Criteria for Buying & Holding Stocks

A working checklist for evaluating whether a stock is worth buying and holding for the long term. No stock will score perfectly on all of these — weight what matters most for the specific business (moat and management for compounders, balance sheet for cyclicals, valuation discipline always).

## Business Quality

1. **Durable competitive advantage** — brand, network effects, switching costs, scale, IP
2. **Pricing power** — can it raise prices without losing customers?
3. **Recurring or predictable revenue**
4. **Industry tailwinds** — growing TAM vs structural decline
5. **Customer concentration** — exposure if a top client leaves
6. **Disruption risk** — from technology or new entrants

## Financial Health

7. **Revenue growth** over 5–10 years (consistency > peaks)
8. **Earnings and free cash flow growth**
9. **Return on invested capital (ROIC)** — ideally above cost of capital, sustained
10. **Operating margins** and the trend
11. **Debt levels** — net debt/EBITDA, interest coverage
12. **Share count trend** — net buybacks vs dilution from stock comp
13. **Cash conversion** — FCF as % of net income

## Management & Governance

14. **Capital allocation track record** — acquisitions, buybacks, reinvestment
15. **Insider ownership** and recent insider buying
16. **Compensation structure** — tied to long-term value or short-term stock price?
17. **Honesty** in shareholder letters and earnings calls
18. **CEO/CFO tenure** and succession planning

## Valuation

19. **P/E vs growth rate (PEG)** and vs historical range
20. **Free cash flow yield**
21. **EV/EBITDA** vs peers
22. **Reverse-DCF** — what growth rate is the price implying, and is it realistic?
23. **Margin of safety** vs estimate of intrinsic value

## Risk

24. **Regulatory or political exposure**
25. **Cyclicality** — sensitivity to interest rates / commodities
26. **Geographic and supplier diversification**
27. **Red flags** — litigation, accounting issues, auditor changes

## Personal Fit

28. **Circle of competence** — can you explain the business simply?
29. **Position sizing** — small enough to hold through 50% drawdowns
30. **Tax situation and time horizon match**

---

*Not financial advice — a starting framework, not personalized recommendations.*
